# Named Techniques Registry

Reusable, portable techniques elevated from the skill flow. Each has been given a named entry because it's a pattern that recurs across product contexts.

---

## 1. The 2-Step Commitment Pattern
**Source:** M2L3 — Interpret (Growth.Design)
**Phase:** Act → Store
**Pattern:** Surface a small, easy request first (email for a free resource), then follow with the larger ask (paid subscription) after the user has invested. Works because Commitment and Consistency makes the second step feel identity-aligned.
**When to use:** Any conversion flow where the primary ask feels too large for a first interaction.
**Anti-pattern:** Making the first step trivially small (just a click) — the commitment must involve enough investment to shift identity.

## 2. The Humane Notification Reset
**Source:** M5L1 — Ethics & Humane Design
**Phase:** Ethics
**Pattern:** Audit every notification your product sends. For each: (1) Does the user need this? (2) Does the user want this? (3) Does this serve the user or our engagement metric? Cut anything where the answer to #3 is "our metric."
**When to use:** Quarterly notification audit. Any time a new notification type is proposed. Any time engagement metrics are healthy but sentiment is declining.
**Anti-pattern:** Cutting all notifications — some are genuinely valuable. The test is user-need, not volume.

## 3. Turn "Likes" into "Works"
**Source:** M4L1 — Communicate Product Decisions
**Phase:** Communicate
**Pattern:** When a stakeholder gives subjective feedback ("I don't like the blue"), redirect from preference to effectiveness: "What about the blue isn't working for the goal of this page?" Shifts from unarguable opinion to resolvable problem.
**When to use:** Any stakeholder review where feedback is framed as personal preference.
**Anti-pattern:** Using this to dismiss legitimate aesthetic feedback — sometimes the color genuinely is wrong.

## 4. The 3-Step Feedback Formula
**Source:** M4L1 — Communicate Product Decisions
**Phase:** Communicate
**Pattern:** (1) Acknowledge the concern, (2) Explain the reasoning connected to user need, (3) Offer a bounded alternative that doesn't break the design.
**When to use:** Any design review. Any feedback session. Any time a stakeholder pushes back on a design decision.
**Anti-pattern:** Skipping step 1 (acknowledgment). Without it, the explanation sounds defensive.

## 5. B.I.A.S. for Relationships
**Source:** M4L1 — Communicate Product Decisions
**Phase:** Communicate
**Pattern:** Apply the B.I.A.S. framework to your stakeholder instead of your user. What can't they see? How will they interpret your proposal? What's the smallest action you can get? What will they remember?
**When to use:** Preparing for any meeting where design decisions need approval. Especially useful with the HiPPO (Highest-Paid Person's Opinion).

## 6. The Postal Clerk Analogy
**Source:** M3L1 — Map Your Customer Journey
**Phase:** Journey Map
**Pattern:** Visualize each touchpoint as a counter in a post office. At each counter, the user either gets helped efficiently (Jump) or fills out another form (Drop). Three consecutive "form" counters and they leave the building — even if counter #4 would have solved everything.
**When to use:** When walking stakeholders through a journey map. Makes abstract Psych Level tangible and relatable.

## 7. Loss-Framing Escalation
**Source:** Synthesized from M2L3 (Loss Aversion) + M2L5 (Store) + applied pattern
**Phase:** Interpret → Store
**Pattern:** Match loss-framing intensity to user investment level:
- **Low investment** (< 2 minutes): Convenience frame — "Pick up anywhere"
- **Medium investment** (2–10 minutes): Soft loss frame — "Your changes are waiting"
- **High investment** (10+ minutes): Direct loss frame — "Don't lose your work"
Using heavy loss framing at low investment feels like nagging and triggers reactance. Using soft framing at high investment undersells the risk.
**When to use:** Any save/recovery prompt system. Any escalating notification sequence.

## 8. The Background Check
**Source:** M4L1 — Communicate Product Decisions
**Phase:** Communicate
**Pattern:** Before presenting a design decision, research the stakeholder's current priorities, recent wins, active concerns, and preferred communication style. Present the design in their context, not yours.
**When to use:** Before any high-stakes presentation. Essential for unfamiliar stakeholders.

## 9. Stakeholder Empathy Questions
**Source:** M4L1 — Communicate Product Decisions
**Phase:** Communicate
**Pattern:** Mirror the GEQ structure for stakeholders: "What outcome are you hoping for?" / "What's your biggest concern about this?" / "What would make you comfortable shipping this?"
**When to use:** At the start of any design review session. Surfaces the real objection before you present.
